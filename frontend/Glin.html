Glycemic Index

Learn how the glycemic index affects blood sugar and how to make better choices for steady energy

What is the Glycemic Index?

The Glycemic Index (GI) ranks carbohydrates based on how quickly they raise blood glucose levels. Foods are rated on a scale from 0 to 100, with higher values indicating faster blood sugar spikes. High-GI foods are rapidly digested, while low-GI foods provide slower, more sustained energy.

Why Does GI Matter?

A diet focused on low-GI foods can help regulate blood sugar, reduce the risk of type 2 diabetes, and improve energy levels. High-GI foods can lead to energy crashes and hunger shortly after eating. Choosing the right carbs supports better metabolic health.

Examples of GI Ratings

Low GI (55 or less): Lentils, oats, sweet potatoes, most fruits.
Medium GI (56-69): Brown rice, couscous, bananas.
High GI (70+): White bread, instant rice, sugary drinks.

Glycemic Index vs Glycemic Load

While GI measures the quality of carbs, Glycemic Load (GL) considers quantity. GL gives a fuller picture of how a portion of food will affect blood sugar. For example, watermelon has a high GI but low GL due to its high water content.

GI Impact on Athletic Performance

Athletes may choose high-GI foods before or after intense workouts for rapid energy or recovery. In contrast, low-GI meals are ideal for endurance activities, providing stable energy over time.

GI Awareness for Better Health

Reading labels, understanding whole foods, and pairing carbs with protein or fat can moderate GI effects. Fiber-rich, less-processed foods generally rank lower on the GI scale and are healthier choices overall.
